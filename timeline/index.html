Moses

Moses' Birth

During a time of great oppression, the Pharaoh of Egypt ordered that all newborn Hebrew boys be thrown into the Nile to control the growing Israelite population. Jochebed, a Hebrew woman, gave birth to a son and, seeing his beauty and promise, hid him for three months. When she could no longer keep him hidden, she placed him in a basket made of reeds and set it afloat on the Nile, trusting God to protect him. His sister, Miriam, watched from a distance, hoping for a miracle.

Miriam

As Moses floated down the river, his sister Miriam, a young girl, watched over him with care and courage. When Pharaoh's daughter discovered the baby and showed compassion, Miriam boldly approached and offered to find a Hebrew woman to nurse him. Though just a child, she played a crucial role in God's plan, ensuring that Moses remained connected to his true heritage. Miriam was likely at an age where she was still a virgin, similar to Mary, the mother of Jesus, who was also a young girl chosen for a divine purpose.

Second in Command

Moses was raised as an Egyptian prince in Pharaoh’s palace, receiving the finest education and training in leadership. As he grew, he gained status and authority, becoming a respected figure in Egyptian society. Though he lived among royalty, he never forgot his Hebrew heritage. Eventually, he rose to a high position, possibly second only to Pharaoh himself, gaining influence and power.

Stepping Down

Moses, once a powerful figure in Pharaoh’s palace, chose to leave behind his position and privileges to stand with his oppressed people. He could no longer ignore their suffering, and in a defining moment, he defended a fellow Hebrew, which led to his exile from Egypt. This sacrifice mirrors the journey of Jesus, who stepped down from heaven to live as a humble man among humanity. Just as Moses left his royal status to lead his people to freedom, Jesus left His divine glory to save mankind.

Ten Commandments

10 Commandments

Moses received the Ten Commandments from God on Mount Sinai. These laws were written on stone tablets and given to the Israelites as a covenant between them and God.
